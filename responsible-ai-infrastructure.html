Subject 01 / Public-review draft

AI infrastructure should prove what it consumes, changes, and returns.

Data centers, cloud systems, and energy-intensive AI can affect water, electricity, household bills, jobs, data rights, and local ecosystems. This draft asks what evidence and protections should be required before public-benefit claims are made.

Start the pressure test Read the formal draft

Public review only. This page does not certify, endorse, approve, or authorize an infrastructure project.

In plain language

The proposal in one minute

AI infrastructure should not ask the public to trust broad promises. Before an actor can claim alignment, affected people should be able to inspect the likely costs, the promised safeguards, the decision rights, and the evidence that will remain public.

01 / Measure

Show the likely impact

Publish credible estimates for grid demand, water use, household costs, jobs, data, and ecology.

02 / Govern

Give affected people a role

People who carry the risk should have meaningful authority, not only a listening session.

03 / Protect

Set pause and repair rules

Define what would require mitigation, redesign, delay, or rejection before construction or scaling.

04 / Prove

Leave a public record

Use independent review and publish non-sensitive evidence so claims can be checked over time.

01

Energy + grid

Can the grid carry the load without shifting risk to the public?

What could go wrong?
New demand could weaken reliability, delay cleaner power, or shift infrastructure costs onto households.
What proof is needed?
A public grid-impact study, demand assumptions, reliability safeguards, and a clear plan for who pays.
Who needs protection?
Ratepayers, vulnerable households, local utilities, workers, and communities already carrying energy burdens.
When should it pause?
When the project cannot show reliable supply or prevent unreasonable public cost and service risk.

Formal public draft

Minimum requirements under review

Status: public-review one-pager. The language below is the source draft rendered for easier reading; it is not a final standard or certification scheme.

Core principle

AI infrastructure should not raise bills, drain water, weaken grids, extract data, displace workers, or burden ecosystems without transparent evidence, affected-party governance, and measurable community benefit.

01

Energy and reliability

Grid-impact analysis, reliability safeguards, and a resilience contribution.

02

Household-bill protection

No modeled net harm for vulnerable households without mitigation or benefit routing.

03

Water

Water-use disclosure, basin-specific thresholds, drought sensitivity, and public reporting.

04

Community benefit

A binding community benefit agreement with participatory governance.

05

Workforce and students

Local training, transition benefits, no-surprise automation, and career pathways.

06

Telemetry and data

Public-interest telemetry governed through a data trust or equivalent accountability.

07

Ecological accountability

Baseline ecological review, restoration triggers, and Rights-of-Nature alignment where legally viable.

08

Audit

Independent review, a public dashboard, a claims register, and evidence publication.

09

No-go conditions

Defined conditions that pause, redesign, or reject the project.

Affected-party governance

People who carry the risk have a meaningful role in decisions, oversight, and remedy.

Minimum evidence threshold

The least proof that must exist before an actor can make an alignment or public-benefit claim.

Public record

A non-sensitive receipt showing what was measured, promised, decided, delivered, and audited.

What alignment should not mean

It should not mean the project is certified, the community approved it, ecological risks are solved, data may be used for model training, or reviewers endorse the project.
