# Common Ground Standard for Responsible AI Infrastructure

Status: Public-review one-pager
Date: June 13, 2026

## Purpose

The Common Ground Standard defines minimum public-interest requirements for AI-enabling infrastructure, including data centers, cloud infrastructure, energy-intensive AI deployments, and related community impacts.

## Core Principle

AI infrastructure should not raise bills, drain water, weaken grids, extract data, displace workers, or burden ecosystems without transparent evidence, affected-party governance, and measurable community benefit.

## Minimum Requirements

| Domain | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| Energy and reliability | Grid-impact analysis, reliability safeguards, and resilience contribution |
| Retail-bill protection | No modeled net retail-bill harm for vulnerable households without mitigation or benefit routing |
| Water | water-use disclosure, basin-specific thresholds, drought sensitivity, and public reporting |
| Community benefit | binding community benefit agreement with participatory governance |
| Workforce and students | local training, transition benefits, no-surprise automation, and career pathway support |
| Telemetry and data | public-interest telemetry governed through data trust or equivalent accountability |
| Ecological accountability | baseline ecological review, restoration triggers, and Rights-of-Nature alignment where legally viable |
| Audit | independent review, public dashboard, claims register, and evidence publication |
| No-go conditions | defined conditions that pause, redesign, or reject the project |

## What Reviewers Should Test

- Are these requirements specific enough to prevent greenwashing?
- What minimum evidence should be mandatory before any alignment claim?
- What should trigger a no-go condition?
- Who should be allowed to review or audit compliance?
- What community rights are missing?
- What energy, water, labor, student, or ecological risks are understated?

## What Alignment Should Not Mean

Alignment should not mean:

- the project is certified;
- the community has approved it;
- ecological risks are solved;
- data can be used for model training;
- a company may use public-benefit language without evidence;
- reviewers endorse the project.
