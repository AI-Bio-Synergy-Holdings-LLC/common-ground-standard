In plain language

A useful review in one minute

A strong review does not need to sound technical. It identifies a real concern, explains who is affected, states what evidence or protection is missing, and recommends a concrete change.

01 / Choose

Start from experience

Pick the campus, worker, community, rights, or institutional lens you know best.

02 / Test

Examine one claim

Ask what could fail, who could be excluded, and what proof would make the claim credible.

03 / Name

Make effects visible

Identify the people, services, rights, places, or resources that carry the risk.

04 / Improve

Propose a usable change

Suggest a clearer sentence, stronger safeguard, evidence threshold, decision right, or no-go condition.

Formal public guide

Question sets under review

Use these prompts as a menu. Reviewers are invited to answer one or several; completing every question is not expected.

Review principle

Critique should test whether the proposal is clear, complete, feasible, resistant to capture, and accountable to affected people.

01 / Overall proposal

Purpose, scope, and legitimacy

  • What problem does this draft solve well?
  • What important problem or affected group is missing?
  • Which claims are too broad or need clearer evidence?
  • What would make the proposal easier to misuse?
  • What should remain public, independent, or outside implementer control?
  • What would make participation meaningful rather than symbolic?
  • What are the first three changes you would make?
02 / Responsible infrastructure

Evidence, place, and no-go terms

  • Which infrastructure impacts must be measured locally?
  • What baseline is needed for energy, water, land, labor, or service effects?
  • Who needs notice, voice, appeal, or stop authority?
  • What proof should be public before a benefit claim is made?
  • Which harms cannot be traded for a payment or promised benefit?
  • What independent review should be required?
  • What would make a pilot too risky to begin?
03 / Campus governance

Learning, work, data, and authority

  • Are student authorship and learning integrity clear enough?
  • Where should AI use be prohibited or disclosed?
  • What student-data uses require explicit governance?
  • What notice and appeal rights are needed?
  • How should faculty and workers shape procurement and deployment?
  • What safeguards prevent surveillance or hidden discipline?
  • What campus benefit should be visible and reviewable?
04 / Benefit sharing

Measurement, decision rights, and delivery

  • What counts as a real gain?
  • What costs, quality effects, and transferred work must be included?
  • Who should decide how verified gains are used?
  • Which benefit pathways are meaningful in this setting?
  • What protections cannot be waived?
  • What should a public receipt show?
  • When should a benefit claim be withdrawn?
05 / Evidence and assurance

Proof, review, and correction

  • What is the minimum credible evidence for this claim?
  • Who can verify it independently?
  • What should be public without exposing sensitive data?
  • How often should results be reviewed?
  • What correction, repair, or no-go response follows failure?

Claim

A statement about a benefit, safeguard, outcome, or level of alignment that should be tested.

Minimum proof

The least evidence needed before a claim can be treated as credible or shared publicly.

Reputation cover

Language that makes an actor appear responsible without enforceable safeguards or reviewable results.

What participation should not mean

It should not require technical credentials, force reviewers to use GitHub, treat silence as consent, publish private information, or imply that a reviewer endorses the Common Ground Standard or its steward.

One focused contribution

What is the first change that would make this draft more credible?

Explain the concern, who is affected, and the evidence, right, safeguard, or wording you recommend.
