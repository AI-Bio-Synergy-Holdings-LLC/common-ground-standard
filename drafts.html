Common Ground Standard For Responsible AI Infrastructure

Purpose

The Common Ground Standard defines minimum public-interest requirements for AI-enabling infrastructure, including data centers, cloud infrastructure, energy-intensive AI deployments, and related community impacts.

Core Principle

AI infrastructure should not raise bills, drain water, weaken grids, extract data, displace workers, or burden ecosystems without transparent evidence, affected-party governance, and measurable community benefit.

Minimum Requirements

DomainRequirement
Energy and reliabilityGrid-impact analysis, reliability safeguards, and resilience contribution
Retail-bill protectionNo modeled net retail-bill harm for vulnerable households without mitigation or benefit routing
WaterWater-use disclosure, basin-specific thresholds, drought sensitivity, and public reporting
Community benefitBinding community benefit agreement with participatory governance
Workforce and studentsLocal training, transition benefits, no-surprise automation, and career pathway support
Telemetry and dataPublic-interest telemetry governed through data trust or equivalent accountability
Ecological accountabilityBaseline ecological review, restoration triggers, and Rights-of-Nature alignment where legally viable
AuditIndependent review, public dashboard, claims register, and evidence publication
No-go conditionsDefined conditions that pause, redesign, or reject the project

What Reviewers Should Test

  • Are these requirements specific enough to prevent greenwashing?
  • What minimum evidence should be mandatory before any alignment claim?
  • What should trigger a no-go condition?
  • Who should be allowed to review or audit compliance?
  • What community rights are missing?
  • What energy, water, labor, student, or ecological risks are understated?

What Alignment Should Not Mean

Alignment should not mean the project is certified, the community has approved it, ecological risks are solved, data can be used for model training, a company may use public-benefit language without evidence, or reviewers endorse the project.

Campus Common Ground Compact

Purpose

The Campus Common Ground Compact gives students, faculty, campus workers, and universities a practical governance framework for human-centered AI adoption. It treats students as co-governors of the AI transition, not passive users or obstacles.

Core Principle

AI may support learning, research, accessibility, civic planning, and public benefit, but it must not replace human authorship, human judgment, student agency, worker dignity, or democratic governance.

Compact Commitments

CommitmentMeaning
Human authorshipStudents must know when AI is allowed, prohibited, disclosed, or evaluated
Learning integrityAI tools must strengthen explanation, reasoning, debate, and original work
Student data trustStudent prompts, submissions, learning data, advising data, and telemetry cannot be reused for model training without explicit governance
No-surprise automationStudents and workers must know when AI affects grading, advising, admissions, hiring, support, or surveillance
Anti-surveillanceAI adoption must not expand hidden monitoring, profiling, proctoring, or disciplinary automation
Worker dignityAI should reduce burden and improve services, not hide staff cuts or degrade teaching and support
Benefit clauseVerified savings should support students, workers, tuition relief, emergency aid, training, or community benefit
Student governance seatStudents must have review or voting authority in campus AI procurement and deployment

First Pilot Options

  • AI procurement review board
  • Student data trust
  • No-surprise automation policy
  • AI disclosure and authorship rubric
  • Student benefit-routing pilot
  • Campus AI public dashboard

AI Dividend / Benefit Clause

Purpose

The AI Dividend / Benefit Clause ensures that verified AI productivity gains leave a public receipt and route a defined share into visible benefit. It can be adapted for public agencies, universities, nonprofits, care networks, unions, community organizations, and responsible enterprise pilots.

Model Clause Summary

  1. Measure the gain using a documented baseline and method.
  2. Disclose material assumptions and risks.
  3. Provide affected workers, students, service recipients, or community representatives with governance authority over benefit-routing decisions.
  4. Allocate a defined share of verified value into an approved benefit pathway.
  5. Publish a non-sensitive public receipt showing measured value, governance decision, routed benefit, and review status.
  6. Preserve data rights, privacy, labor protections, appeal rights, and human accountability.

Approved Benefit Pathways

  • Direct dividends
  • Emergency aid
  • Bill relief
  • Student support
  • Worker transition funds
  • Training funds
  • Service improvements
  • Cooperative ownership
  • Community resilience funds
  • Ecological restoration
  • Debt defense or debt relief
  • Public-benefit reinvestment

Reviewer Questions

General Questions

  1. What claim in this packet feels strongest?
  2. What claim feels too broad, vague, or premature?
  3. What language sounds like public benefit but could enable capture?
  4. What minimum evidence should be required before any actor can claim alignment?
  5. What should be explicitly prohibited?
  6. What would make this credible to skeptical students, workers, communities, and environmental reviewers?
  7. What would make you worry this could become reputation cover for AI or infrastructure actors?

Evidence Questions

  1. What evidence should be required before outreach to major companies?
  2. What evidence should be required before any public pilot announcement?
  3. What evidence should be required before certification?
  4. What failures should be published?
  5. Who can credibly audit early pilots?

If you had to make this standard harder to capture, what are the first three changes you would make?

Anti-Capture Review Checklist

This checklist helps reviewers identify whether the Common Ground Standard drafts could be captured by powerful actors, diluted into public-relations language, or used without real community, student, worker, data, or ecological accountability.

Governance Capture

  • Does any actor gain control over the standard?
  • Are affected people represented with decision power, not just consultation?
  • Are pause, appeal, or no-go rights clear?

Data Capture

  • Are data rights explicit?
  • Is model training prohibited unless explicitly approved?
  • Is raw sensitive data excluded from public release?

Evidence Capture

  • Are claims tied to baseline evidence?
  • Are failures and limitations publishable?
  • Is endorsement clearly separated from review?

Economic Capture

  • Are affected people involved in benefit-routing decisions?
  • Are benefits more than public relations spending?
  • Are savings measured against a credible baseline?

Infrastructure Capture

  • Are grid, water, and retail-bill impacts independently reviewable?
  • Are no-go conditions enforceable?
  • Are community benefit agreements binding?

Campus Capture

  • Do students have real governance authority?
  • Is hidden surveillance prohibited?
  • Are appeal rights defined?

Common Ground Standard is a public-review draft, not a certification or endorsement process.
